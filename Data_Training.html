Course objective · Module 01 of 04

Sharing data,
protecting people.

Every release of data carries downstream consequences. Where it lands, who else gains access, what auxiliary information it joins with — those dependencies are largely invisible to the originating organization. This module makes them visible.

Upon completion, learners will be able to —
  1. Distinguish between direct identifiers, quasi-identifiers, and sensitive attributes, and assess the relative re-identification risk each carries.
  2. Describe how real-time bidding (RTB) auctions disclose user attributes to third parties in under 100 milliseconds.
  3. Identify the risk indicators that warrant heightened scrutiny in a data-sharing proposal, and the conditions under which release is appropriately justified.
  4. Explain how auxiliary data reverses both anonymized releases and pseudonymous disclosures, drawing on the Strava heat map incident, the Massachusetts medical-records release, and the @PierreDelecto identification.
  5. Select an appropriate level of de-identification and the corresponding transformation technique for a given release scenario.
  6. Apply the WP29 taxonomy (singling out, linkability, inference) and the quantitative measures k-anonymity and l-diversity when assessing a dataset's residual risk.

Information · Foundation concept

Before you can protect data,
you need to know what you're protecting.

"Personal data," as defined under GDPR Article 4(1) and largely paralleled in U.S. state privacy law, means any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person. The privacy field divides personal data into two categories. The majority of data-sharing failures encountered in this module arise from organizations underestimating one of them.

When privacy professionals refer to identifying an individual within a dataset, they do not mean only the obvious attributes. They mean any attribute, or combination of attributes, that can be traced back to a specific natural person. The scope is substantially broader than most people recognize. The two categories below organize every modern privacy program — what each is, how each appears in practice, and why one accounts for the predominant share of re-identification incidents.

Type 01 · Direct identifiers

Identifies a person on its own.

An attribute that, in isolation, points to one specific individual. No combining, no inference, no auxiliary data required.

Examples
Full name Email address Phone number Government ID Account number Passport number Driver's license

These are the readily identifiable attributes. Removal is almost always the first step when releasing data — but rarely the only step required.

Type 02 · Quasi-identifiers (indirect identifiers)

Identifies a person when combined.

An attribute that appears innocuous in isolation. Joined with one or two other apparently innocuous attributes, it identifies an individual with substantial accuracy.

Examples
ZIP code Date of birth Sex Job title Employer Browser fingerprint Device ID Trip history

Quasi-identifiers represent the predominant source of re-identification risk. Their face value suggests trivia; their joint distribution functions as identification. The remainder of this module is, in essence, an inventory of techniques for managing them.

87%

The finding that reshaped the field.

Sweeney (2000) demonstrated that ZIP code, date of birth, and sex alone are sufficient to uniquely identify approximately 87% of the U.S. population — by name, by address, and by all associated records. None of those three attributes was considered "identifying" under prevailing practice. The finding directly informed the HIPAA Safe Harbor rule (45 CFR §164.514(b)(2)), which now restricts release of the very fields she used.

Most data-sharing failures do not result from a leaked name. They result from organizations treating quasi-identifiers as innocuous metadata and releasing them without transformation. Two documented cases later in this module illustrate the pattern — one involving a state governor's full medical record, one involving a sitting U.S. senator's pseudonymous social-media account. Both began with attributes no one considered identifying.

The classification is now visible.

The direct identifiers were those already pointing to an individual — full name, email, phone, government IDs. The quasi-identifier category accounts for the majority of re-identification incidents in documented breaches. Browser fingerprint, ZIP code, date of birth, job title — none identifies an individual alone. All combine with each other, and with auxiliary data, to do exactly that.

One additional category is worth noting: "sensitive personal data" (also termed "special category data" under GDPR Article 9) — attributes for which the consequences of disclosure are disproportionately high, such as health records, financial accounts, real-time location, and biometric data. This is a parallel framework that overlaps with both categories above. The module returns to it in the section on anonymization techniques.

When a webpage loads an ad,

In under a second, user attributes pass through several companies — most undisclosed to the data subject. Correct. The disclosures at each step:

The full sequence executes in approximately 100 milliseconds — roughly one-third the duration of a human blink. The participants: Publishers are the websites or apps the user visits. SSPs (supply-side platforms) are the exchanges that monetize publisher inventory. DSPs (demand-side platforms) are the buyers bidding for ad placement.

  1. The user loads a webpage or app.The browser already carries substantial user context — cookies, location signals, prior interaction history. None is surfaced to the user, but all is about to be transmitted.
  2. The publisher loads its ad slots.Each ad placement triggers a request chain. The user's cookie ID, the page URL, and device characteristics are packaged and sent to the publisher's SSP.
  3. The SSP constructs a bid request.The request aggregates the available user attributes — year of birth, gender, location, device, behavioral history — into a single message, then broadcasts that message simultaneously to dozens or hundreds of DSPs.
  4. DSPs evaluate the impression.Each has under 100 milliseconds to decide whether to bid. The principal exposure: every DSP receives the bid request, including those that do not bid. Receipt of the request is independent of intent to purchase the impression — and several participants extract data without ever bidding.
  5. The winning ad renders.The auction is invisible to the user. By the time the page completes loading, the user's attributes have been received and retained by every participant in the auction — the majority of which are not known to the user.

A heat map for runners
was a map of the bases.

In 2018, the fitness application Strava released a global heat map aggregating user activity. The visualization received widespread praise on release; it also constituted a significant operational security incident. U.S. service members had been logging runs around military installations across multiple OCONUS theaters — Syria, Afghanistan, and Somalia among them. The company's position. The governance principle.

Strava's position

The company emphasized that the heat map aggregated activities users had elected to share publicly, and that individual users retained the option to mark activities private or opt out of the aggregated dataset entirely.

The principle

The defense is technically accurate and substantively inadequate. When an organization designs the product, the organization is accountable for the privacy outcomes — not the user located several layers deep in a settings interface they may never reach.

One additional principle: data, once released, cannot be retracted. By the time the patterns were identified externally, they had already been catalogued, captured, and redistributed. The activity sessions ended; the disclosures persisted.

Not all data sharing
carries the same risk.

Some releases constitute high-severity privacy exposures. Others — municipal traffic analyses, public-safety enforcement — are reasonable, sometimes necessary. Distinguishing the two is the operational question for any data-sharing review. Sort each scenario into the appropriate category.

The pattern is consistent.

The heightened-risk releases share four risk indicators: real-time precision, individual identifiability, absent consent, and absent downstream controls on the receiving party. Any single indicator warrants escalation; any combination warrants substantive review. Particular attention is owed to releases that incidentally identify third parties — family, contacts, neighbors — who never participated in any consent process.

The justified releases consistently apply two GDPR-codified principles: purpose limitation (data scoped to a specific stated use) and data minimization (no more attributes than the use requires). Municipal traffic analyses, parking enforcement, and prohibited-zone detection meet both criteria. The dataset is structured to serve one defined function rather than to become a general-purpose surveillance feed.

Click through · Two documented cases

"Anonymous" data isn't
when someone has the other piece.

Removing identifiers rarely removes the individual. Two documented cases demonstrate how auxiliary data — a public voter list, a publicly visible social network — was sufficient to fully reverse a release described as "anonymized." Click each step to advance the narrative.

01
In the mid-1990s, the Commonwealth of Massachusetts released a state-employee medical database for research purposes, characterized as "anonymized." Names, addresses, and telephone numbers had been removed. The Governor publicly assured constituents that patient privacy had been preserved.
02
Graduate researcher Latanya Sweeney observed that three quasi-identifiers had been retained in the dataset: ZIP code, date of birth, and sex. She purchased the publicly available voter registration list for $20. The same three attributes appeared there as well.
03
She executed a join across the two datasets on those three attributes. Only one record in the medical data matched the Governor's ZIP code, date of birth, and sex. She extracted his complete medical record — diagnoses, prescriptions, hospital visits — and mailed it to his office.
04
Sweeney's subsequent research (Sweeney, 2000) demonstrated that approximately 87% of the U.S. population can be uniquely identified using ZIP code, date of birth, and sex alone. The finding directly informed the HIPAA Safe Harbor rule (45 CFR §164.514(b)(2)), which today restricts the release of those very attributes.

The principle. The data was not anonymous; it was de-identified — and inadequately. Three attributes the Commonwealth deemed harmless, joined to a publicly available voter list, identified the most senior elected official in the state. The patient was not protected by the data; the patient was protected by the absence of an attacker.

01
In 2019, Senator Mitt Romney disclosed to The Atlantic that he maintained a pseudonymous Twitter account. He used it to monitor political discourse without attribution.
02
In passing, he noted that the account followed 668 other accounts. That single quantitative attribute — combined with the disclosure that the account existed — constituted the entire identifying signal.
03
Journalist Ashley Feinberg worked through the public social-media presence of Romney's adult family members to map the network of accounts a hidden Romney account would plausibly follow. She then filtered Twitter accounts on the expected attribute profile: roughly 668 follows, an interest in political commentary, signals of Mormon-cultural affiliation, and traces of his French missionary tenure.
04
One account satisfied every filter. The handle: @PierreDelecto — combining his French missionary tenure with a private reference. Romney confirmed the account within hours.

The principle. Romney never disclosed his handle. He disclosed three attributes: account existence, follower count, and topical interests. His family, in public, supplied the remainder — without recognizing that their social-media networks functioned as a queryable index. The data was not leaked; it was assembled from auxiliary sources.

Three levels · Pick a tab to explore

There isn't one anonymization.
There are three.

Each successive level reduces privacy risk and increases operational cost. Most organizations do not select a level deliberately; they arrive at one by default. The three levels below align to the de-identification taxonomy used in NIST SP 800-188 and ISO/IEC 20889. Click each tab to examine what each level does — and what residual risk each leaves behind.

Replace direct identifiers and retain a key.

Names, email addresses, and identifiers are substituted with synthetic equivalents. The mapping table linking real values to synthetic values is stored separately under stricter access controls. The original data is not eliminated — it is held behind a lookup. This corresponds to the GDPR Article 4(5) definition of pseudonymization.

Pseudonymization is the lightest form of de-identification and the most reversible — sometimes by design. Use cases include longitudinal medical research, fraud investigation, and any scenario requiring re-identification capability.

Residual risk. Compromise of the mapping table — through breach, legal compulsion, or vendor disclosure — re-identifies every record at once. Pseudonymization does not reduce privacy risk; it concentrates it in a single artifact.

Remove direct identifiers and generalize quasi-identifiers.

The most common operational meaning of "anonymized" data. Direct identifiers are deleted outright. Quasi-identifiers — ZIP code, date of birth, job title, location — are transformed using suppression (attribute removal), generalization (coarsening to broader buckets), substitution (categorical replacement), and perturbation (calibrated noise addition). Mapping keys are destroyed rather than retained.

Executed rigorously, this provides meaningful protection. Executed inadequately — as in the Massachusetts release — it preserves enough quasi-identifier structure that auxiliary data can still reverse the de-identification.

Residual risk. Auxiliary data can still link records. A voter file, a breach corpus, a public follower list — any external dataset can reduce a generalized record to one individual. Partial anonymization is bounded by the assumptions made about what an attacker could plausibly hold.

Partial anonymization plus a linkability audit.

All transformations from partial anonymization, augmented by an active audit: could any plausible adversary, using any plausibly accessible data — public records, social-media graphs, breach corpora, government files, commercial data brokers — link their data to yours and re-identify a record?

This is the rigorous standard, and the rare practice. Verification is slow, costly, and never permanent: each new public dataset published tomorrow can invalidate today's assumptions.

Click to open · Five techniques

Five transformation techniques
for de-identifying data prior to release.

Once the contents of a dataset are characterized, the operational question becomes which transformations to apply. The five techniques below — drawn from the standard taxonomy in ISO/IEC 20889 — represent the most common approaches to reducing identifiability while preserving analytical utility. None is sufficient in isolation. Open each to examine how it operates and where it falls short.

01
Suppression · attribute removal

The most direct technique. Identify the attributes that name an individual — full name, email, phone, government IDs — and delete them prior to release.

Suppression is almost always the first transformation applied. It is almost never the only one required.

Example
name: "Tonja Ryan", email: "tr@x.com"
name: ▢▢▢, email: ▢▢▢
Limitation — The retained quasi-identifiers (ZIP, date of birth, job title) frequently re-identify the individual when joined with auxiliary data.
02
Substitution · synthetic data generation

Real names, addresses, and identifiers are replaced with plausibly structured synthetic equivalents. Useful when the dataset must retain its schema and structural properties — for example, in test environments where empty fields would cause downstream failures.

Example
901 Main St, Austin TX
442 Oak Ln, Newark NJ
Limitation — Generating convincing synthetic data at scale is non-trivial. Distributional patterns from the original dataset (frequency, co-occurrence) often persist and remain analytically recoverable.
03
Perturbation · noise addition

Calibrated noise is added to numeric attributes. Heights are adjusted by an inch or two. ZIP codes shift to a neighboring code. Trip times round to the nearest half-hour. Each individual record is slightly inaccurate, but aggregate statistical properties remain analytically useful.

Example
trip: 12:22 PM → 1:09 PM
trip: 12:30 PM → 1:00 PM
Limitation — Excessive noise destroys utility; insufficient noise can be filtered out statistically. In small datasets, auxiliary data can still re-identify individuals despite the perturbation.
04
Permutation · record swapping

All values are retained but reassigned across records. Alice's salary becomes associated with Bob's record and vice versa. Aggregate distributions remain identical; the linkage between an individual and a specific value is severed.

Example
Alice: $98K · Bob: $45K
Alice: $45K · Bob: $98K
Limitation — Where only one individual matches a particular description (the sole CEO, the sole intern), the associated value is recoverable regardless of how it was permuted.
05
Generalization · aggregation

Specific values are replaced with broader categorical buckets. Age 47 becomes "40–50." ZIP 78712 becomes "central Texas." Each record carries less specific information, and identification of any individual record becomes substantially more difficult.

Example
age: 47 · ZIP: 78712
age: 40–50 · ZIP: 787**
Limitation — Generalization alone provides only partial protection. It typically must be paired with another technique — a point demonstrated quantitatively in the next screen.
Three risks · Click each to expand

Three ways a record
can still be re-identified.

Before releasing a dataset, three categories of residual risk must be evaluated. Each represents a distinct pathway by which a record nominally stripped of identifiers can still be linked to an individual. The framework comes from WP29 Opinion 05/2014 and remains the working taxonomy used by EU data protection authorities.

RISK 01
Singling out
Can any record in the dataset be isolated to one specific individual?

A record is "singled out" when its attributes uniquely match exactly one individual. The Massachusetts release singled out the Governor through three quasi-identifiers. Many "anonymized" datasets contain records that single out their subject — frequently without the publisher recognizing it.

How to detect: Count how many records share each combination of quasi-identifiers. Any combination matched by exactly one record represents a singling-out risk.

Mitigation — Generalize quasi-identifiers until every record shares its combination with at least k other records. This is the operational definition of k-anonymity, examined on the next screen.
RISK 02
Linkability
Can records in this dataset be joined to records in another dataset to re-identify individuals?

Linkability is the risk that an external dataset — public records, social media, a breach corpus, a commercial broker file — shares enough attributes with the released dataset to permit a join. Sweeney's work on the Massachusetts release demonstrated linkability against the voter list. Feinberg's identification of @PierreDelecto demonstrated linkability against public social graphs.

How to detect: Enumerate the auxiliary datasets a plausible adversary might possess. For each, identify the shared attribute set. Any non-trivial shared set warrants escalation.

Mitigation — Suppress or generalize the shared attributes specifically. Add calibrated noise to numeric quasi-identifiers. Document the linkability audit as part of the release decision.
RISK 03
Inference
Can a sensitive attribute about an individual be inferred from the dataset without re-identifying that individual directly?

The most subtle of the three. A release can satisfy both singling-out and linkability protections and still permit damaging inferences. If every individual in ZIP 90210 carrying a particular set of quasi-identifiers also carries a particular diagnosis, an adversary who knows the quasi-identifiers can infer the diagnosis — regardless of whether they can name the patient.

How to detect: For each group of records sharing the same quasi-identifier combination, examine the distribution of sensitive attributes. Concentration in a single value signals inference risk.

Mitigation — Require diversity within each group of records sharing quasi-identifiers. This is the operational definition of l-diversity, examined two screens forward.

A quantitative measure
for "not singled out."

If quasi-identifiers re-identify individuals, the operational countermeasure is to generalize those quasi-identifiers until each record is indistinguishable from a sufficient number of others. The minimum group size at which this holds is called k. A dataset satisfies k-anonymity when every combination of quasi-identifiers appears in at least k records. The notion was formalized by Sweeney (2002).

The operational threshold.

The widely cited working threshold is k = 5: every combination of quasi-identifiers appears in at least five records. Below this threshold, individual records can frequently be singled out by an adversary. Above it, singling-out becomes substantially more difficult — though, as the next screen shows, it does not exhaust the residual risk.

What went wrong. The inference loophole.

Each individual record is k-anonymous: any one cannot be singled out. But every record in the group carries the same diagnosis. An adversary who knows that a particular individual — a 45-year-old woman in central Austin — appears in this dataset can infer her diagnosis with 100% certainty, without ever identifying her record specifically.

This is the precise risk Machanavajjhala et al. (2007) identified, and the reason l-diversity was proposed as an extension to k-anonymity.

What changed. Diversity within the group.

Each group of k indistinguishable records now contains at least l distinct values for the sensitive attribute. With l = 3, an adversary who knows that a 45-year-old woman in central Austin appears here can no longer infer her diagnosis. The probability of any specific diagnosis is bounded by the diversity within the group.

L-diversity does not eliminate inference risk entirely — subsequent measures (t-closeness, Li et al. 2007; differential privacy, Dwork 2006) refine it further. But it captures the core operational principle.

The synthesis. Two axes, two measures.

K-anonymity addresses singling out. L-diversity addresses inference. A defensible release must evaluate both axes — and increasingly, must evaluate linkability against the universe of plausibly accessible auxiliary data as well.

Module synthesis

Seven principles
from this module.

The framework summarized here applies whenever data is being prepared for release, sharing with a third party, or use in a context other than the one in which it was originally collected.

  1. Personal data is broader than direct identifiers. Quasi-identifiers — ZIP, date of birth, sex, job title, device fingerprint, trip history — account for the majority of documented re-identification incidents.
  2. "Anonymized" is a claim, not a property. A release described as anonymized may, in fact, be pseudonymized (reversible), partially anonymized (reduced risk but linkable), or fully anonymized (linkability-audited). The three are not interchangeable.
  3. Auxiliary data is the principal threat surface. Public voter lists. Public social graphs. Breach corpora. Data-broker files. Most documented re-identifications were not data leaks — they were joins against external data the releaser did not consider.
  4. Release decisions must address three risk pathways. Singling out, linkability, and inference (WP29 taxonomy). A release that addresses only one of the three remains exposed on the other two.
  5. Quantitative measures provide audit standards, not guarantees. K-anonymity and l-diversity bound singling-out and inference risk respectively. They do not eliminate either, and they do not address linkability at all.
  6. Once released, data cannot be retracted. The Strava patterns were catalogued externally within hours of publication. Privacy decisions are effectively one-way.
  7. Privacy is an organizational accountability, not a user setting. Designs that defer protection to a settings interface the user may never reach are not, in any meaningful sense, designs that protect users.

The bottom line.

"Personal data" is not a category of attributes. It is a relationship between any attribute, in any combination, and the individual to whom that combination ultimately points. Protect the relationship — not just the obvious labels.
