What is SI?

Before you can lead a session, you need to understand the program you’re part of — what it is, where it came from, who it serves, and where you fit. This page is the longest in the module by design. Read it carefully.

The Foundation

SI is built around the course, not around struggling students

Supplemental Instruction is a peer-led academic support program. Unlike tutoring, which targets individual students who are falling behind, SI targets historically difficult courses — courses where 30% or more of students typically earn a D, an F, or withdraw. The intervention is at the course level, and it’s open to every student enrolled.

Your sessions are weekly, voluntary, and free. They’re held outside class time, structured around peer collaboration, and built on the premise that students who recently completed the course are uniquely positioned to help current students succeed in it.

Where SI came from

A short history — because context matters.

1973
UMKC

Developed by Dr. Deana Martin at the University of Missouri–Kansas City

Dr. Martin designed SI specifically for medical and dental school courses where high failure rates were costing universities (and students) enormous amounts of time and money. Her insight was that the problem wasn’t the students — it was the way historically difficult courses were taught. Adding more lectures wouldn’t help; what was needed was structured peer-driven practice with the actual course material.

Today SI is recognized by the U.S. Department of Education as an exemplary program and is used at hundreds of universities worldwide. At UT Austin, it’s housed in the Sanger Learning Center and supports more than 40 undergraduate courses across 13 colleges. You’re part of a 50+ year tradition that has measurably changed student outcomes.

The research is real

Decades of data, replicated across institutions.

That cognitive proximity is the engine of SI: you remember which concepts confused you, which study strategies actually worked, and what the professor's testing patterns look like.

Proactive, not remedial

Tap to reveal

Proactive, not remedial

SI is not triage. It’s designed to build durable learning skills before exam panic hits — which means high-achievers benefit just as much as students struggling. The student earning an A who attends SI to consolidate their understanding is using the program exactly as intended.

Facilitated, not lectured

Tap to reveal

Facilitated, not lectured

The whole point is to design situations where students do the thinking. Your job is to ask, redirect, and check for understanding — not to explain. If you find yourself talking for more than 30 seconds at a stretch, something has slipped. The room produces the answers; you surface them.

Collaborative

Students learn from each other, not from you.

Tap to reveal

Collaborative

Real understanding shows up when students explain concepts to each other. Your activities are built around peer-to-peer talk, not Q&A with the leader. The student who teaches a concept to their classmate retains it dramatically better than the student who just heard it explained — your structure makes that teaching happen.

Who shows up — and why

SI is for everyone in the course. Here’s who you’ll actually see.

01
The Catch-Up
Bombed exam 1 and is shaken

Showed up to the first SI session after a rough first exam. Wants to figure out what went wrong and how to study differently. Most likely to come back next week.

02
The Steady B
Wants an A and is willing to work for it

Doing fine in the course but knows they need more practice to push their B into A territory. Often the most engaged participant — they ask the questions other students were thinking.

03
The Top of Class
Already getting an A — there to consolidate

Comes to teach others their understanding (which deepens it). Don’t make them do all the work — pair them with quieter students and let the explaining flow naturally.

04
The Returner
Took this course before, retaking it now

Often the most strategic about how they use SI — they know which topics will trip them up. Acknowledge their experience without singling them out, and let it inform the questions they bring.

You won’t know who is who when they walk in — and you don’t need to. Treat every student as a thinker who has something to contribute, and design activities that work for all four. The "Steady B" and "Top of Class" students will pull the "Catch-Up" student forward without you doing anything heroic.

Why a peer (and not a TA, professor, or expert tutor)?

The cognitive proximity principle

You just struggled through this course. That is your superpower.

A professor knows the material so well they’ve forgotten what it’s like not to know it. A TA may be a year or two ahead of where students are. You sat in that exact same seat last semester, asked the questions they’re asking now, and came out the other side. You remember what it was like to not get it — that memory is what makes peer-led work.

Your job isn’t to know more than your students. Your job is to remember how you got there and to design experiences that let your students get there too.

So what exactly do you do?

Five roles you’ll wear, often in a single session.

01
Facilitator
— not a lecturer

You design questions and activities, then get out of the way. The room generates answers; you guide the structure.

02
Activity Designer
— not a worksheet generator

You build the lesson plan each week — opener, main activity, closer. The activity should put students in the driver’s seat.

03
Confusion Detector
— not a grader

You listen for what students don’t understand. That data shapes next week’s session and gets passed up to your supervisor.

04
Connector
— not a counselor

You connect students to each other (peer study partners), to resources (Sanger, library, writing center), and lightly to the professor.

05
Model Student
— not a perfect one

You model good study habits, intellectual honesty ("I don’t know — let’s find out"), and how to talk about confusion without shame.

What SI is not

Common misconceptions, busted. The myth is struck through; the actual is below it.

×
SI is NOT tutoring
SI is a group, peer-led program. Tutoring is one-on-one expert help. Different format, different goal.
×
SI is NOT a homework help session
You are not there to walk students through their assigned problem sets. You design lessons that teach transferable strategies — students apply those strategies to their homework on their own.
×
SI is NOT for failing students only
SI is for any student in the course. The students who benefit most are often the ones already doing okay — they’re using the structure to consolidate.
×
SI is NOT an emergency service
SI is a weekly support structure, not a one-off rescue before the exam. The grade improvements in the program data come from students who attend consistently across the semester — not from students who show up once when they’re panicking. Frame it that way when you pitch.
×
You’re NOT a TA
You don’t grade, take attendance, or run office hours. You don’t have access to student grades. You report to your supervisor at Sanger, not to the professor.
×
You should explain things students didn’t understand from lecture
You design activities through which they teach themselves and each other. Re-lecturing the lecture is the failure mode of new SI Leaders — it’s the easiest way to fall into.
×
A good SI session means everyone got the right answer
A good SI session means students did the thinking, surfaced their confusion, and left with a strategy they didn’t have when they arrived. Sometimes that means leaving with productive uncertainty.

Mindsets to walk in with

Internalize these before your first session. Come back to them mid-semester when you’re tired.

"I am not the smartest person in this room. The room is."
Together, your students will out-think you. Your job is to design the structure that lets that thinking happen — not to demonstrate that you know more than they do.
"Silence is thinking, not failure."
When you ask a hard question and the room goes quiet, your gut will tell you to fill the space. Don’t. The silence is when the actual learning is happening.
"My job is to design good questions, not to give good answers."
If you find yourself answering, ask why. Could the question have gone back to a peer? Could a different question have surfaced the misunderstanding instead?
"Confusion is data, not failure."
When a student is confused, it’s a signal — about which concept is hard, which language doesn’t land, which prerequisite is shaky. Welcome it. Track it. Use it.
"I don’t have to be perfect. I have to be present."
A session where you’re fully attentive but make a small content mistake is better than a polished session where you’re going through the motions. Students can always tell the difference.

SI is not passive learning

Many students arrive at SI expecting a tutor to walk them through answers. A large part of your role is actively resisting that dynamic. The goal is for students to discover, reason through, and confirm knowledge on their own and with each other — not to receive it from you.

Passive learning
What SI is not
  • Students sit and listen while a leader explains content
  • The leader is the authority — students wait to be told
  • Learning depends on the quality of the person at the front
  • Students leave relying on someone outside themselves
SI
Active, peer-driven learning
  • Students work through problems collaboratively — you facilitate
  • The group is the resource — peers teach each other
  • Learning depends on the quality of the group's thinking
  • Students leave trusting their own ability to find answers

Foundational Techniques

These three foundational techniques will be your muscle memory as an SI Leader. Once they’re automatic, you can spend your attention on the room instead of on yourself. Tap each card to expand — and after the matching activity, you’ll see a curated set of additional moves you can layer on top.

01
Technique One
Wait Time (Think-Pair-Share)
What it is

After asking a question, pause deliberately for 5–10 seconds before calling on anyone. In Think-Pair-Share, students think alone, then discuss with a partner, then share with the group.

Why it matters

It equalizes participation, gives introverts time to formulate answers, and dramatically improves response quality. Most instructors wait less than one second — a brief pause changes everything.

Common challenge

The silence feels uncomfortable. You’ll want to fill it. Resist the urge.

When to pivot

If no one responds after a full wait, rephrase the question or offer a scaffold, then redirect again.

02
Technique Two
Redirecting Questions
What it is

When a student asks you a question, bounce it back to the group: “That’s a great question — what does everyone else think?” or “Who can help answer that?”

Why it matters

It keeps the session student-centered. Students learn more when they arrive at answers themselves. It builds group cohesion and reduces dependence on the SI Leader as the answer source.

Common challenge

Students expect you to just tell them. Some may be frustrated at first — that’s okay. Stay consistent.

When to pivot

If the question involves a factual error or logistics that genuinely require a direct answer from you, give it concisely, then return to facilitation mode.

03
Technique Three
Check for Understanding (Clusters)
What it is

Regularly verify that the group truly understands before moving on. Use targeted questions, quick polls, clusters (small-group checks), or exit tickets.

Why it matters

Students often think they understand until asked to explain or apply a concept. Real-time checks reveal hidden gaps and let you adjust the session on the fly.

Common challenge

Students nod and say “yes” even when confused. Use open-ended checks — “Can you explain that in your own words?” — not yes/no questions.

When to pivot

If time is short, condense checks to one targeted question rather than skipping them entirely.

Engaging ways to check for understanding

The fastest way to know if students get it — without anyone having to raise their hand alone.

06 First to Five
Heads-down finger rating — anonymous comprehension check.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Students put their heads down and hold up 1–5 fingers. 5 = "I completely understand," 1 = "I still don't get it." Quick scan of the room before deciding what to do next.
08 Stop Light
Red / yellow / green reflection on each topic covered.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Students reflect on each agenda topic: RED ("you lost me at..."), YELLOW ("I could use clarification on..."), GREEN ("I now know that..."). Surfaces exactly what each student needs reinforced.
07 Snowball
Anonymous question crumple-and-toss across the room.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Students write a content question or concept they don't understand on paper, crumple and toss it. Students take turns reading the random questions aloud and the group works through them together.
25 Brain Dump
Blank-paper write-everything-you-remember exercise.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Students take a blank sheet and write everything they remember from the session within a time limit. Compare with peers, or collect to assess what stuck.
60 Two Truths and a Lie
Identify the true statement among two falsehoods.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Prepare two false statements and one true on a topic. Students identify the true one. Discuss the false ones — why they're false and how to make them true. Great mirror of multiple-choice exam questions.
29 One-Minute Paper
Brief written reflection in students' own words.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Students write a sentence or paragraph on a topic. Emphasize own thoughts, not polished writing. Use as an exit ticket, or have students share in pairs.

Pulling every voice into the room

For when participation is uneven or the room has gone quiet.

15 Three Before Me
Students must ask three peers before asking the SI Leader.
Tap for how-to
How it works
When asked a question, send the student to three peers first. If all three are problematic, that's a signal to review the topic with the group. Reinforces peer-as-resource.
09 Ask the Right Students
Random name selection prevents one or two from dominating.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Have every student write their name on a folded slip. Pull names randomly to determine who answers next. Put the name back so students don't go complacent.
61 Verbal Volleyball
Toss-and-name: each catch = a new concept from the unit.
Tap for how-to
How it works
In a circle, one student tosses a ball and names a concept. The catcher names a new one. Can't repeat. Continues until names run out (8–10 minutes), then discuss any concepts missed.
46 Send a Problem
Rotating problems passed left, building partial solutions.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Each pair starts with a different problem. After a minute, pass it right. Continue until all problems are solved by the room collectively. Group review at the end.
47 Speed Dating
3-minute rotating pair discussions on questions or prompts.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Pair students. 3-minute rounds: share responses on a prompt; partner gives feedback. After each round, one stays, the other rotates. Everyone talks to several peers in a short time.
51 Around the World
Quick-recall race in a circle.
Tap for how-to
How it works
Sit in a large circle. Two students stand for a question; first correct answer "travels" to the next student. Continue until someone has gone fully around. Fast, energetic, and works as a fun review.

Your In-Class Pitch

In the first or second week, you’ll stand up in front of the class you’re SI-ing for and explain what you do, when you’ll be there, and — just as important — what SI isn’t. The in-person ask gets dramatically better attendance than a Canvas post alone. This page builds you a 60-second script you can rehearse and send to yourself.

Why the pitch matters

Students will form their mental model of SI in the first 60 seconds you speak

If your pitch sounds like “office hours but with a peer,” they’ll treat it like office hours. If it sounds like a tutoring session, they’ll only show up when they’re failing. The single biggest job of the pitch is to set the right mental model: collaborative, peer-driven, problem-focused, twice a week, identical sessions, bring your notes.

The frame: what SI is, what it isn’t

Both columns matter. The “isn’t” half is what protects you from the wrong students showing up expecting the wrong thing.

SI is
  • Collaborative. Students work problems together, with each other.
  • Built around the lectures. What gets covered each week tracks what your professor is teaching.
  • Notes-driven. Students bring their lecture notes and use them to work through practice problems.
  • Twice a week, same content. Two identical sessions — students pick whichever fits their schedule.
  • Voluntary and free. Open to every student in the course, no signup needed.
  • For everyone. Students with an A benefit just as much as students who are catching up.
SI is NOT
  • A substitute for office hours. Bring conceptual questions to your professor.
  • A substitute for lecture. SI assumes you’ve been to class — it’s not a recap.
  • A lecture. The SI Leader is not standing at the front delivering content.
  • A TA session. Your SI Leader doesn’t grade, take attendance, or have access to your scores.
  • One-on-one tutoring. The room is the resource, not just the leader.
  • The leader walking through every problem. Students drive the work; the leader facilitates.

Anatomy of a 60-second pitch

Four beats. Keep it tight — students tune out fast.

01
Hook + who you are
Name, year, major, and that you took this exact course recently. Establishes peer credibility — you sat where they’re sitting.
~10 sec
02
What SI is — the positive frame
Two sessions a week, identical, built around the lectures. Collaborative, problem-focused, notes-driven. Pick the one that fits.
~20 sec
03
What SI is NOT — the boundary
Not a lecture. Not office hours. Not a tutor at the front of the room. Sets expectations so the right students show up for the right reason.
~15 sec
04
Where, when, and the close
Both session times and locations, posted on Canvas, see you there. Warm, low-pressure, and brief.
~15 sec

Delivery tips

A few small things that separate a flat pitch from one students remember.

1
Stand where the professor stood. Don’t pitch from your seat. Walk to the front, face the room, and own the space — even if it’s only for a minute.
2
Rehearse out loud, not in your head. Read the generated script aloud three times before class. Your mouth needs to know the shape of the words; your brain alone won’t be enough.
3
Make eye contact with three different parts of the room. Front-left, back, front-right. It signals you’re talking to them, not at them.
4
Slow down on the “isn’t” beat. The “what SI isn’t” section is where students mentally place you. Rushing it means they default to the closest mental model they have — which is usually “tutor” or “TA.”
5
Frame it as a weekly habit, not a backup plan. Say “every week” early and often. The students who see real grade gains are the ones who attend consistently across the semester — not the ones who panic-show-up the week before the exam. Set that expectation on day one.
6
End with the times, not your name. The last thing students hear is the thing they’re most likely to remember. Make it the day, time, and location — not “thanks, I’m so-and-so.”
7
Smile, even if you’re nervous. A 60-second pitch is short enough that the room will mirror your energy. Warmth in your voice is worth more than polish in your wording.

Your Professor as Partner

Your professor isn’t your boss and you aren’t their TA — but you are their partner in supporting students this semester. The relationship works best when you treat it that way: brief, warm, professional, and free of pressure.

Read this first

The two ends of the spectrum

Some professors will share exam topics with you, ask for your weekly lesson plan, and add SI to their syllabus. Others will say "thanks, you run it however you want, no need to coordinate." Both are equally fine. Your job is to introduce yourself, set up a clear way to reach you, and let them choose their level of involvement. Don’t push.

The do’s and don’ts

Tap each card to expand.

Do
✓
Keep it brief — three short paragraphs is plenty
Professors are busy. A long email reads as needy or unfocused. Three paragraphs: who you are, your sessions, an open door for collaboration. That’s it.
✓
Welcome collaboration when offered
If they offer to share exam topics, practice problems, or content for your weekly post — say yes. That insight helps you serve their students better. Set up a clear cadence so it doesn’t become last-minute.
✓
End with a clear open door
Your email and phone, plus an explicit invitation: "Reach out anytime if you’d like to coordinate, share materials, or just stay in touch." Make it easy for them to engage when they want to.
Don’t
×
Pressure them for more involvement
If they say they’re hands-off, accept it. Don’t follow up asking for more meetings, more shared materials, or more access. Their boundary is information — respect it and run great sessions anyway.
×
Ask for class time without offering value
Don’t ask for a five-minute pitch slot in their lecture unless you’re bringing something concrete — a quick intro video, a poll, a one-page handout. Vague asks erode the relationship.

Anatomy of an SI Lesson

A good SI session has a shape: a low-stakes opener that gets the room talking, a main activity built around the hardest content of the week, and a closer that lets you see what stuck. Tap each phase below to expand.

Opener≤5 min
Main Activity30–35 min
Closer10–15 min
5 min

Opener · Lower the temperature

A low-stakes way to ease students into the room — never a forced round-robin. The goal is to lower anxiety, not raise it. Pick a move from the icebreaker bank below.

Why it matters

A room that hasn't spoken at all in the first five minutes will stay quiet for the rest of the session. But forcing every student to speak does the opposite of what you want — it spikes anxiety, especially for introverts and students new to peer-led work. The opener should make the room feel warmer without making anyone feel exposed.

Two flavors that work
  • Passive — students engage while arriving (Padlet via the attendance QR, a live poll on screen, a sticky-note prompt). No one has to speak. By the time you start, everyone has already participated.
  • Quick volunteer — a casual question where 1–2 people chip in and you move on. "Anyone doing anything fun this weekend?" gets the room laughing without forcing anyone to share.
What to avoid
  • Round-robin sharing. "Let's go around and everyone say…" is the single biggest anxiety spike in new SI sessions.
  • Deep or vulnerable questions. "Tell us something interesting about yourself" lands badly when students don't know each other yet.
  • Long openers. Five minutes is a ceiling, not a floor. If 1–2 people answer and the room feels lighter, you're done.
Leader's role

If you ask a question, answer it yourself first — briefly — so students see what "short and casual" looks like. Take 1–2 volunteers, smile, and pivot into the content. Don't survey the room.

30–35 min

Main activity · Where the learning happens

A peer-driven activity built around the hardest content of the week. Students do the thinking; you facilitate. This is the longest phase by design.

What it looks like

Students in pairs or small groups working through a problem, debating an answer, sorting examples, walking through a process aloud. The room should sound busy with peer talk — not silent with you talking.

Common formats
  • Sequencing or sorting cards (cut up examples, students put them in order)
  • Worked-example contrast (correct vs. flawed, identify the error)
  • Jigsaw — each pair becomes "expert" on one part, then teaches another pair
  • Concept map or whiteboard build-out
Leader’s role

Circulate. Listen. Ask probing questions. Don’t give answers. Wait time is your superpower in this phase — let groups wrestle with uncertainty before redirecting.

10–15 min

Closer · Check for understanding

Verify what stuck before students leave. A targeted question, an exit ticket, or a quick poll. Not optional.

Why it matters

Students often think they understand until asked to explain. The closer is your last chance to surface gaps — and your data point on whether the activity worked.

Examples
  • "Each person writes one sentence connecting today’s activity to a study strategy they’ll use this week."
  • "Restate the key concept to your partner in your own words."
  • "On the way out, drop one question you still have on the index card by the door."
Leader’s role

Listen for gaps. If multiple students miss the same thing, plan to address it next session. Your closer feeds your next opener.

Prompts that land

Why it works

Quick volunteer

One question, 1–2 voices, move on

Never round-robin. The point is to make the room feel warm — not to survey it.

"Anyone doing anything fun this weekend?"
The classic. Costs nothing, lands every time.
Tap for how-to
How to run it

Ask casually as students settle in. Answer your own question first ("I'm trying that new ramen place"). Take 1–2 volunteers. Smile, react genuinely, and pivot. Total time: under a minute.

Variations
  • "Anyone do anything fun this past weekend?" (Monday version)
  • "Anyone got plans for break?"
  • "Anything good on TV right now?"
What NOT to do

Don't go around the room. Don't call on anyone who didn't volunteer. If no one bites, share something of your own and pivot — that's fine too.

"One word for this week"
Low-stakes check-in that doesn't require explanation.
Tap for how-to
How to run it

"Give me one word for how this week's going." Say yours first ("survival"). Wait. Two or three people will chip in. Don't push for more.

Why it works

One word is doable for anyone — even students who'd never share a full thought. The room hears multiple voices in 20 seconds. You can read the temperature from the words you hear.

Show of hands questions
Zero speaking required. Hands go up, you read the room.
Tap for how-to
How to run it

Pose a casual either-or or yes-or-no. Count hands. Make a comment about what you see. Move on.

Prompts that land
  • "Hands up: who's caffeinated right now?"
  • "Quick poll — Team Jacob or Team Edward?"
  • "Who already started studying for the next exam? Be honest."
  • "Who's taken a class with this professor before?"
"Anyone watch/read/play anything good?"
Open invitation. Whoever wants to share, does.

How to run it

Ask once. Take 1–2 volunteers. React with curiosity ("oh nice, what's that one?"). Note: this can spiral if you let it — keep the cap firm and pivot.

When to use it

Best mid-semester when students already know each other a bit. On day one it can be too open-ended.

Quick warm-ups that point at the work

Connects the room to the day's content without launching into it. Use these once students are comfortable.

Have one problem already on the whiteboard or first slide when students arrive. Sign on it: "Try this with your neighbor while we wait." Students start working immediately — no formal "begin" needed.

Students are doing collaborative problem-solving before they realized the session started. Particularly good for pre-exam reviews.

Connect course content to something happening in the world.

How to run it

If something course-relevant is in the news (a scientific finding, a relevant event, a viral explainer), open with a one-line mention. Take 1–2 quick reactions. Note that the content this week connects.

When it works

Most powerful for STEM where the link to the real world isn't always obvious from the textbook. Less needed for humanities where students already make those connections.

What to avoid

  • Round-robin "everyone introduce yourself." The single biggest anxiety spike in new SI sessions. Students start mentally rehearsing instead of listening.
  • Deep or vulnerable questions on day one. "Tell us something interesting about yourself" lands badly when students don't know each other. Save this for week four if at all.
  • Calling on specific students who didn't volunteer. Use random call-on only for content questions, never for icebreakers.
  • Long openers. Five minutes is a hard ceiling. If you've talked for two minutes and only you have spoken, abort and move on.
  • The same prompt every week. Students notice. Rotate across categories and keep it fresh.

Pairs vs. small groups

When in doubt, smaller is usually better. Here’s how to decide.

Use pairs when
Pairs (2 students)
  • The activity is short (under 10 min) and high-focus
  • Every student needs a turn to talk
  • The task involves explaining something to one other person
  • Quiet students need scaffolded participation
  • You want to maximize time-on-task per student
Use small groups when
Small groups (3–4 students)
  • The activity benefits from multiple perspectives
  • You want to assign roles (manager, scribe, reporter, reflector)
  • The problem is complex enough to need division of labor
  • Students will present out to the larger group at the end
  • You want to surface disagreement and have students resolve it

Lesson 01 · MCAT Prep

Sequencing for the MCAT

50 minutes · Group-based · MCAT prep
Learning Objective Students will be able to identify and apply sequential ordering strategies to MCAT-style passages by physically manipulating story fragments, collaboratively reconstructing narrative logic, and transferring that reasoning process to an authentic MCAT excerpt.
Icebreaker5 min
Group Activity20 min
Debrief12 min
Application13 min
5 min

Opener · Icebreaker check-in

"What is one thing you watched, read, or listened to this week that you’d recommend?"

What students do

Each student shares for 20–30 seconds. No right or wrong answer — reduces anxiety before academic content begins.

What the leader does

Sit with the group, not at the front. Share your own answer first — keep it casual, model peer-level disclosure (not academic content). Notice who hesitates and gently invite them in: "I’d love to hear yours when you’re ready." Don’t push; if a student passes, that’s fine — come back to them next week.

Sample warm follow-ups
  • "Oh interesting — what made it stand out for you?"
  • "I haven’t seen/read that one — can you tell us a bit about it?"
  • "That’s a fun choice — anyone in the group have something similar?"
How does this support SI?

It signals "this room talks." A student who has spoken once in the first five minutes is far more likely to speak when the content gets hard.

20 min

Group activity · Cut-up story sequencing

SI leader pre-cuts 3–4 short narratives into individual sentence strips, all shuffled together in an envelope per group.

Part A · Sort by story (8 min)

Small groups (3–4 students) sort strips by which story each belongs to, justifying choices aloud.

Part B · Sequence within stories (7 min)

Groups arrange each story’s strips in correct order. Every member must be able to explain the final sequence.

What the leader does while groups work

Circulate group to group, but resist the urge to hover. Listen for 30–60 seconds before speaking. Notice which groups have settled too fast (they’re probably guessing) versus which are arguing productively. When you do speak, ask one question and move on — let them sit with the difficulty for at least 30 seconds before returning. Carry a small notebook and jot down the smartest student-generated reasoning you overhear; you’ll surface it in the debrief.

Questions to ask while circulating
  • "What’s your group’s reasoning for putting that strip first?"
  • "I notice you disagreed there — walk me through both sides."
  • "If we swapped these two strips, what would change about the meaning?"
  • "What signal word or phrase tells you that’s the order?"
  • "Has anyone considered what happened in the sentence just before this one?"
  • "Which strip did your group spend the longest on? Why?"
How does this support SI?

Physical manipulation slows down the cognitive process. Students can’t just speed-read; they have to argue for ordering choices, which surfaces the reasoning patterns the MCAT will test.

12 min

Whole-group debrief · Sequencing strategies + MCAT connection

Bring the full group back. Discussion is facilitated — not lectured.

What the leader does

Stand near the board, not in front of it. Write only what students generate — not your own additions. After asking a question, fold your hands and wait at least 5 full seconds before doing anything else. When a student answers, redirect to the group: "Who agrees with that? Who would push back?" If you find yourself talking more than 30 seconds at a stretch, that’s your cue to redirect.

Key questions to open the debrief
  • "What cues did your group use to decide which strip came first?" (hold the silence)
  • "Did your group disagree? How did you resolve it?"
  • "Where in the MCAT might you need to sequence information like this?"
Sample follow-up redirects
  • "Can someone from another group restate that in their own words?"
  • "What’s a counterexample to that strategy?"
  • "When have you had to use this same skill in another class or another exam?"
  • "If this were a 600-word passage instead of 5 sentences, would your strategy still work?"
What to capture on the board

Record 2–3 student-generated strategies in their own words. Don’t paraphrase — attribution matters. The board at the end of debrief should be visible proof that the group solved it.

13 min

Application · MCAT excerpt — paired sequencing practice

SI leader distributes a real MCAT-style passage requiring sequential reasoning.

What students do

Pairs annotate together — identifying sequence shifts, cause-effect, and chronological signals. Each pair answers 1–2 targeted questions, then shares one insight with the group.

What the leader does while pairs work

Move between pairs every 60–90 seconds. Don’t sit with one pair too long — it signals "I’ll rescue you" to the others. When a pair looks stuck, ask one redirecting question and walk away. Let them sit with the difficulty before you return. If you spot a strong insight, ask that pair if they’d be willing to share it back to the room — builds confidence and seeds the closer.

Questions to ask while circulating
  • "Which sentence felt like the strongest sequence cue?"
  • "Walk me through how you decided what came first."
  • "If I gave you a longer passage, would your strategy change?"
  • "What’s the trickiest moment in this passage for you?"
  • "Have you tried the strategy your group landed on earlier?"
Check for understanding

"Can someone restate what that pair found in different words?" — the gold-standard CFU prompt. Don’t accept "yeah, I get it" as an answer.

Wrap-up

Each student writes one sentence connecting today’s activity to a study strategy they’ll use this week. The leader collects these as informal exit tickets and uses them to plan next session’s opener.

Step C · 8 min

Design your main activity

Most main activities are 1–3 phases long. Phase A is the heart of your activity — phases B, C, and D are optional, only add them if your activity genuinely breaks into distinct steps. Each phase should put students in the driver's seat — your role is to facilitate, not present.

The last few minutes. How will you know whether the session worked? Skip "any questions?" — that's a yes/no check that always returns yes. Use something with output you can read.

Submit & Feedback

How submission works

Your supervisor reads every plan before it runs

You’ll submit your draft lesson plan to your supervisor by the deadline you set in your schedule (— pick one in Section 3.2 —). Your supervisor reads it, leaves written feedback, and sends it back. You revise. Then you facilitate.

Revision is part of the work. A first draft that comes back with comments is not a failed plan — it’s a plan that just got better. The leaders who improve fastest are the ones who treat feedback as collaboration, not correction.

What your supervisor is looking for

Five criteria. Tap each to expand.

01
Clear, action-based learning objective Tap

The objective should use a Bloom’s verb and tell you what students will be able to do by the end of the session — not what they’ll have "covered." If your objective starts with "students will understand" or "students will know," it’s probably too vague to test.

02
Activities require peer interaction, not just leader presentation Tap

If a phase reads "I will explain X" or "I will walk them through Y," it needs to be reframed as something students do together. Your supervisor will flag any phase where you’re the active one and students are passive.

03
Each phase has a defined facilitation technique Tap

Wait time, redirecting, or check-for-understanding — every phase should call out which technique you’ll lean on. This shows you’re thinking about the moment-to-moment, not just the activity flow.

04
The closer actually checks for understanding Tap

Not "I’ll ask if anyone has questions" — that’s a yes/no check that always returns yes. Instead: an open-ended prompt, a written response, an exit ticket, or a peer-restate. Something with output you can read.

05
Time adds up to your session length Tap

Sounds basic. It’s the most common feedback in the first month. Add up the minutes for opener + main + closer; if the total is more than your session, something has to give. Most new leaders over-plan; assume each phase will take 30% longer than you think.
